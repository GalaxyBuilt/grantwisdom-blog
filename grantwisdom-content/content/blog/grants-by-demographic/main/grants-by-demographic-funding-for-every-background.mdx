# Grants by Demographic: Funding Opportunities for Every Background and Identity

*The grant funding landscape increasingly reflects the reality that different communities face different barriers and deserve targeted support. Demographic-specific grants exist to ensure that women, people of color, veterans, people with disabilities, youth, seniors, immigrants, and LGBTQ+ individuals and organizations have access to funding tailored to their specific needs and strengths. This guide explores the major demographic funding categories and how to navigate them.*

## Why Demographic-Specific Grants Exist

Demographic-targeted grants are not preferential treatment — they are corrective investment. Decades of research demonstrate that certain communities face systemic barriers to accessing capital, education, healthcare, and economic opportunity. Grant programs designed for specific demographics acknowledge these disparities and work to level the playing field.

For funders, demographic-specific programs allow them to direct resources with precision — ensuring that historically underinvested communities receive support rather than competing on an uneven field against more resourced organizations.

For applicants, demographic-specific grants offer a more aligned playing field, language that reflects your community's experience, and funders who have made an explicit commitment to understanding your context.

Understanding which demographic categories you qualify under — and how to compellingly articulate your demographic identity in grant applications — is a strategic funding asset.

## Grants for Women and Women-Led Organizations

Women entrepreneurs, researchers, artists, and nonprofit leaders have access to a growing and substantial ecosystem of grants specifically designed to address gender-based funding gaps.

**Small Business Grants for Women**: The federal Women's Business Centers (WBCs) program provides technical assistance and connects women entrepreneurs to financing, but a variety of private grant programs offer direct capital to women-owned businesses. Amber Grant Foundation, IFundWomen, Tory Burch Foundation, and SBA-linked programs are among the most prominent.

**Women in STEM**: Foundations and government programs fund women pursuing careers in science, technology, engineering, and mathematics, where gender gaps remain significant. AAUW fellowships, Microsoft Research grants, and NSF programs with gender equity components are examples.

**Women's Health and Reproductive Rights**: Foundation funding for women's health organizations, maternal health initiatives, and reproductive health access is substantial. Funders include the Susan T. Buffett Foundation, the Ms. Foundation for Women, and the Women's Funding Network member foundations.

**Women Leaders and Entrepreneurs of Color**: At the intersection of gender and race, several foundations specifically fund women of color-led organizations and entrepreneurs, recognizing the compounding barriers at this intersection. The Fearless Fund, the Akonadi Foundation, and the Women's Foundation of California are examples.

## Grants for Black, Indigenous, and People of Color (BIPOC)

The racial wealth gap, historic disinvestment in communities of color, and ongoing structural barriers have driven growing investment in BIPOC-specific grant programs across the philanthropic, government, and corporate sectors.

**Black-Led Organizations**: Major philanthropic investments in Black-led nonprofits have increased significantly in recent years. Foundations like the Ford Foundation, the W.K. Kellogg Foundation, and numerous community foundations have dedicated funding streams for Black-led community organizations. The Black Voices for Black Justice Fund and similar rapid-response funds emerged during periods of civil unrest and continue to operate.

**Indigenous and Native American Communities**: Tribal nations and Native-led organizations have access to both federal tribal set-asides and foundation funding dedicated to Indigenous sovereignty, cultural preservation, land rights, and Native-led community development. The First Nations Development Institute, the Seventh Generation Fund, and the NDN Collective are important funders in this space.

**Latino and Hispanic Organizations**: Hispanic-serving organizations and Latino-led nonprofits access funding through the Hispanic Foundation of Silicon Valley, the National Council of La Raza, and foundation programs specifically supporting Latino community development, immigration services, and leadership development.

**Asian American and Pacific Islander (AAPI) Organizations**: AAPI-focused funders include the Asian Americans/Pacific Islanders in Philanthropy (AAPIP) network, the Asian Community Fund at the San Francisco Foundation, and foundations that emerged in response to the surge in anti-Asian hate crimes. Government programs increasingly include AAPI-specific components.

**BIPOC Entrepreneurs**: Federal and state small business grant programs increasingly include racial equity components or dedicated BIPOC entrepreneur tracks. Organizations like the LISC Small Business Relief Fund, the Minority Business Development Agency (MBDA), and various state MBDA Business Centers provide targeted support.

## Grants for Veterans and Military Families

Veterans represent a demographic group with specific needs, strong community identity, and substantial dedicated funding — from both government sources and private philanthropy.

**Small Business Grants for Veterans**: The SBA's Office of Veterans Business Development, Veteran Business Outreach Centers, and private programs like the Warrior-Scholar Project and Bunker Labs provide resources and, in some cases, direct grants to veteran entrepreneurs.

**Veterans' Health and Mental Health**: VA programs, foundation funding, and community-based organizations support veterans' physical and mental health needs. Mission 22, Give an Hour, and the Bush Institute's Veteran Employment Initiative are examples of organizations that both fund and provide services.

**Veterans Housing and Homelessness**: HUD-VASH (Veterans Affairs Supportive Housing) is the primary federal program for veteran homelessness, but foundation and nonprofit grants also fund local housing stability and supportive services for veterans.

**Veterans Arts and Healing**: Arts-based programs for veterans have grown substantially, with NEA, state arts agencies, and foundations like the Robert R. McCormick Foundation funding art therapy, theater, writing, and other creative healing programs for veterans and military families.

## Grants for People with Disabilities

Disability-focused grant programs support both organizations serving people with disabilities and individuals with disabilities pursuing education, employment, and creative goals.

**Independent Living and Accessibility**: Centers for Independent Living (CILs) are funded through HHS and provide services to people with disabilities. Federal programs through the Administration for Community Living (ACL) support disability services, assistive technology, and aging and disability resource centers.

**Disability Arts and Culture**: Many arts funders have specific initiatives for artists with disabilities. VSA (Very Special Arts), the Kennedy Center's disability arts programs, and state-level initiatives support creative expression by artists with disabilities.

**Disability Entrepreneurship**: Several programs support entrepreneurs with disabilities, including Disability:IN's programs, the Office of Disability Employment Policy (ODEP) at DOL, and foundation programs focused on disability inclusion in the workforce.

**Special Education and Transition**: IDEA (Individuals with Disabilities Education Act) is the primary federal funding vehicle for special education, but competitive grants support innovative approaches to transition programming, inclusive education models, and autism services.

## Grants for Youth and Young Adults

Young people accessing grants are typically doing so through one of three pathways: scholarships, youth-serving organization grants, or emerging young professional fellowships.

**Youth Development Programs**: Foundations and government programs fund after-school programs, youth mentorship, gang prevention, juvenile justice alternatives, and leadership development for young people. The OJJDP (Office of Juvenile Justice and Delinquency Prevention) and private funders like the Annie E. Casey Foundation are major sources.

**Youth Entrepreneurship**: Programs like the Thiel Fellowship, the Diamond Challenge, and various local startup competitions provide grants and support to young entrepreneurs under 25.

**Youth Arts and Culture**: Youth arts programs have dedicated funding from NEA, state arts agencies, and foundations focused on creative youth development. Programs serving youth in underinvested communities receive particular philanthropic interest.

## Grants for Seniors and Older Adults

The rapidly growing older adult population has created expanded funding for programs serving seniors — from healthcare and housing to employment and civic engagement.

**Aging Services**: The Older Americans Act (OAA) is the primary federal funding vehicle for aging services, administered through the Administration for Community Living (ACL). Area Agencies on Aging (AAAs) receive these funds and subgrant to local organizations providing nutrition, transportation, caregiver support, and other services.

**Senior Economic Security**: Programs funded through ACL, AARP Foundation, and foundations addressing senior poverty support economic security programming for older adults.

**Senior Health and Wellness**: CDC, NIH, and foundation programs fund healthy aging initiatives, fall prevention, chronic disease management, and cognitive health for older adults.

## Grants for LGBTQ+ Individuals and Organizations

LGBTQ+-focused grant funding has grown substantially, driven by increased philanthropic commitment to LGBTQ+ equity and the ongoing need to address disparities in health, economic security, and safety.

**LGBTQ+ Organizations**: The Arcus Foundation, the Gill Foundation, the Human Rights Campaign Foundation, and the Astraea Lesbian Foundation for Justice are among the major funders of LGBTQ+-led organizations and LGBTQ+-focused initiatives.

**LGBTQ+ Health**: HIV/AIDS services remain significantly funded through Ryan White CARE Act programs and foundation support. Broader LGBTQ+ health initiatives address mental health, substance use, and healthcare access disparities.

**LGBTQ+ Youth**: Trevor Project, It Gets Better Foundation, and dozens of local organizations serving LGBTQ+ youth receive foundation and government funding for crisis services, mentorship, and community building.

## Maximizing Your Demographic Funding Eligibility

Most organizations and individuals qualify for multiple demographic funding categories — and smart funders recognize intersectionality. Maximize your demographic funding potential by:

- Clearly identifying all relevant demographic categories in your profile and proposals
- Telling a specific, honest story about how your demographic identity shapes your work and your community's needs
- Building a portfolio of demographic-specific funders across multiple categories
- Staying current on new demographic-specific funding initiatives, which launch frequently in response to current events and social movements

Your identity is a strength. Own it fully in your funding strategy.
