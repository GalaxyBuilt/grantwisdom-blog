# Grants by Industry: Find Funding Specific to Your Sector

*Not all grants are created equal — and not all grants are available to everyone. Industry-specific grants target businesses, organizations, and individuals within defined sectors, offering funding that is more aligned, more targeted, and often less competitive than broad-based programs. This guide explores the major industry verticals with active grant ecosystems and shows you how to find the right funding for your field.*

## Why Industry-Specific Grants Matter

When people think about grants, they often picture generic government programs or broad foundation initiatives. But some of the richest — and least competitive — grant opportunities are deeply sector-specific. These grants exist because funders understand that different industries have different needs, different barriers, and different opportunities for transformative impact.

Industry-specific grants come from government agencies with sector mandates (like the USDA for agriculture, or the Department of Energy for clean tech), from trade associations, from foundations with sector focus areas, and from corporations seeking to invest in the industries they operate within.

For applicants, the advantage of sector-specific grants is alignment. When you apply for a healthcare grant as a healthcare organization, or an agricultural grant as a farmer, you're not trying to shoehorn your work into a generic funding framework. You're speaking directly to funders who understand your world — and that shows in every section of your application.

## Technology and Innovation Grants

The technology sector has one of the richest grant ecosystems of any industry, driven by both public investment in innovation and private sector interest in accelerating the next generation of solutions.

**Federal Programs**: The Small Business Innovation Research (SBIR) and Small Business Technology Transfer (STTR) programs are among the largest sources of early-stage technology funding in the United States, distributing over $3 billion annually across 11 federal agencies. These programs are specifically designed for small businesses pursuing R&D with commercial potential.

**State Innovation Programs**: Many states operate their own tech grant programs, often through economic development agencies or technology commissions. These range from proof-of-concept grants for early-stage startups to scale-up grants for companies approaching commercialization.

**Corporate Innovation Grants**: Companies like Google, Microsoft, and Amazon operate grant programs for tech-enabled social impact, AI innovation, and cloud computing projects. These are typically tied to ecosystem development goals and often include non-monetary resources like cloud credits, mentorship, and technical support.

**Sector-Specific Tech Grants**: Within technology, sub-sector funding exists for cybersecurity, health tech, agricultural tech (agtech), edtech, fintech, and clean tech — each with its own dedicated funders and programs.

## Healthcare and Life Sciences Grants

Healthcare and life sciences represent one of the largest grant ecosystems in the world, with funding flowing from NIH, patient advocacy groups, pharmaceutical foundations, and state health agencies.

**NIH Funding**: The National Institutes of Health is the world's largest funder of biomedical research, distributing over $45 billion annually. NIH grants are available for basic research, clinical trials, training, career development, and center grants. The competition is intense, but the awards are substantial.

**Patient Advocacy Foundation Grants**: Disease-specific foundations — for cancer, diabetes, rare diseases, mental health, and hundreds of other conditions — fund community health programs, research, and patient support services. These are more accessible than NIH grants and often have faster turnaround cycles.

**Community Health Grants**: Federal and state programs fund community health centers, rural health initiatives, substance use treatment programs, and maternal and child health services. HRSA (Health Resources and Services Administration) is a major source of these funds.

**Healthcare Innovation**: AHRQ (Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality) and foundations like the Robert Wood Johnson Foundation fund healthcare systems innovation, workforce development, and health equity initiatives.

## Agriculture and Food Systems Grants

Agriculture has a robust federal grant ecosystem through the USDA, supplemented by state agricultural agencies, rural development programs, and food systems foundations.

**USDA Programs**: The USDA's National Institute of Food and Agriculture (NIFA) funds research, education, and extension programs in agriculture. The Value-Added Producer Grant (VAPG) program supports farmers in developing value-added products. The Beginning Farmer and Rancher Development Program funds training for new agricultural producers.

**Rural Development Grants**: USDA Rural Development offers grants for rural community development, water infrastructure, business development, and housing. These overlap with agriculture but also serve rural communities more broadly.

**Sustainable Agriculture**: Foundations like the Organic Farming Research Foundation, the National Sustainable Agriculture Coalition's member organizations, and regional food systems funders support sustainable and regenerative agriculture practices.

**Food Access and Food Justice**: Foundations and government programs fund urban agriculture, food banks, community gardens, and initiatives addressing food deserts. SNAP outreach, WIC programming, and farm-to-school initiatives all have grant support available.

## Arts, Culture, and Humanities Grants

The arts and culture sector has a dedicated grant ecosystem supported by government arts agencies, private foundations, and corporate giving programs.

**National Endowment for the Arts (NEA)**: The NEA is the primary federal source of arts funding, supporting everything from visual arts to theater, music, literature, and folk arts. Many NEA grants flow through state arts agencies, which then re-grant to local organizations and artists.

**State Arts Agencies**: Every U.S. state has a state arts agency that distributes both NEA pass-through funds and state-appropriated funds. These are often more accessible than direct NEA grants and prioritize local arts communities.

**Foundation Arts Funding**: Foundations with arts focus areas include the Mellon Foundation (arts and humanities), the Doris Duke Charitable Foundation (performing arts), the Kresge Foundation (arts and culture), and hundreds of community foundations with local arts priorities.

**Individual Artist Grants**: Fellowships, residencies, and individual artist grants are available from NEA, state arts agencies, and numerous private foundations. These fund visual artists, writers, composers, choreographers, and other creative practitioners.

## Education and Workforce Development Grants

Education is one of the most actively funded sectors in the philanthropic and government grant landscape.

**Department of Education Programs**: The U.S. Department of Education administers dozens of grant programs covering early childhood education, K-12 innovation, higher education access, special education, and career and technical education.

**Workforce Development Grants**: The Department of Labor's Employment and Training Administration funds workforce development programs through formula grants to states and competitive grants to organizations. The Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act (WIOA) is the primary federal framework.

**Private Foundation Education Funding**: Foundations like the Gates Foundation, the Walton Family Foundation, the Lumina Foundation, and the Joyce Foundation are major funders of education reform, college access, and workforce development.

**Community College and Vocational Training**: Specialized grants support community college programs, apprenticeship development, and vocational training in high-demand fields including healthcare, skilled trades, and information technology.

## Environment and Clean Energy Grants

The environment and clean energy sector has seen explosive growth in grant funding driven by federal climate investments and growing foundation interest in climate solutions.

**Inflation Reduction Act Programs**: The IRA includes significant grant and rebate programs for clean energy, energy efficiency, environmental justice, and climate resilience. Many of these are administered through EPA and DOE.

**EPA Environmental Justice Programs**: EPA's Office of Environmental Justice funds community-based environmental health and justice initiatives in overburdened communities. The Environmental Justice Collaborative Problem-Solving (EJCPS) program is a key example.

**Department of Energy Grants**: DOE funds energy research, clean energy demonstration, grid modernization, and building efficiency through multiple competitive grant programs.

**Environmental Foundations**: Foundations like the Packard Foundation, the Moore Foundation, the Bloomberg Philanthropies, and the Energy Foundation fund conservation, climate advocacy, and clean energy transition work.

## How to Navigate Industry-Specific Grant Research

Finding industry-specific grants requires sector-specific research strategies:

**Start with sector associations**: Your industry's trade association, professional association, or membership organization is often the best curator of relevant grant opportunities. They track funding that affects their members and often promote opportunities through newsletters and databases.

**Monitor sector-specific federal agencies**: Beyond the obvious (NIH for health, USDA for agriculture), many sectors have less-obvious federal funders. Map the agencies that intersect with your work and monitor their grant announcements regularly.

**Follow the foundations**: Research the top 10 to 20 foundations active in your sector. Subscribe to their newsletters, follow their social media, and review their recent 990 filings (available through the Foundation Center) to understand their giving patterns.

The key insight: industry-specific grants are often both more aligned and less competed than general grants. The more precisely you can define your sector — and the more deeply you can speak its language — the better your odds of winning.
