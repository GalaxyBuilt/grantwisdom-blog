# Grants by Location: State, Local, and Regional Funding Opportunities Near You

*One of the most overlooked dimensions of grant seeking is geography. Where you are — your state, your city, your county, your zip code — shapes which grants you're eligible for, how competitive your applications will be, and how much funding is available to you. This guide explores the landscape of location-based grants and shows you how to maximize the geographic dimension of your funding strategy.*

## Why Location Matters in Grant Seeking

Grant funding is deeply geographic. Federal programs flow through state and local agencies, each of which has discretion to prioritize differently. Foundations are often locally focused, prioritizing the communities where their founders lived or where their endowments were built. Economic development initiatives target specific regions based on economic distress designations, enterprise zone boundaries, and opportunity zone maps.

Understanding the geographic dimension of grant funding allows you to:

- Identify state and local programs that have far less competition than national grants
- Leverage your location as a strength — particularly in rural, frontier, or economically distressed areas where funding priority designations open additional doors
- Build relationships with local funders who invest repeatedly in the same communities
- Access pass-through funding that flows from federal programs to state and local agencies

The bottom line: knowing your geography is knowing your funding landscape.

## Federal Grants with Geographic Priority

Many federal grant programs explicitly prioritize or reserve funding for specific geographic areas. Understanding these designations can dramatically expand your eligibility and competitiveness.

**Rural Areas**: Dozens of federal grant programs prioritize rural communities, defined by USDA and other agencies based on population and density thresholds. USDA Rural Development alone administers over 40 programs for rural communities, covering business development, housing, utilities, and community facilities. If you're located in a rural area, this designation is a significant funding asset.

**Economically Distressed Areas**: Designations like Opportunity Zones (created by the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act), HUD Promise Zones, USDA StrikeForce areas, and EDA Economic Development Districts create priority funding access for communities experiencing economic hardship. Organizations serving these areas often receive preference or set-asides in competitive grant programs.

**Urban Areas and Cities**: HUD's CDBG (Community Development Block Grant) program flows to entitlement communities — cities and counties above specific population thresholds — providing flexible funding for community development, housing, and public services. Non-entitlement communities receive CDBG funds through states.

**Tribal Lands**: Many federal programs have tribal set-asides or dedicated funding streams for Federally Recognized Tribal governments and tribal organizations. These include HHS, HUD, DOJ, EPA, and USDA programs with tribal-specific funding.

## State Grant Programs: Your Most Accessible Source

State grant programs are often the most accessible and least competed grant opportunities available. They operate on the front lines of economic development, public health, education, and community services — and they are designed specifically for organizations within their borders.

**State Economic Development Grants**: Every state has an economic development agency (often called the Department of Commerce or Department of Economic Development) that administers grants, loans, and tax incentives for businesses and community development. Programs vary widely but often include small business grants, Main Street revitalization funding, rural business development grants, and workforce training incentives.

**State Arts Agencies**: State arts agencies distribute both NEA pass-through funds and state-appropriated arts funding to local organizations and individual artists. These programs have less competition than direct NEA grants and often have simpler applications. Most state arts agencies also run special initiatives tied to state cultural priorities.

**State Health Departments**: State health departments administer federal public health grants (CDC, HRSA, SAMHSA pass-throughs) and often operate their own state-funded health programs. Priority areas vary by state based on health needs assessment data but commonly include mental health, substance use, maternal and child health, and chronic disease prevention.

**State Environmental Agencies**: EPA formula and competitive grants flow through state environmental agencies for water quality, air quality, brownfields remediation, and environmental justice initiatives. State-level environmental grant programs supplement federal funding with state-specific priorities.

**State Education Agencies**: Title I, IDEA, and other federal education funds flow through state education agencies (SEAs), which re-grant to local education agencies (LEAs). SEAs also administer state-funded education programs and innovation grants.

## City and County Grant Programs

Municipal and county governments are often overlooked as grant funders, yet many cities and counties — particularly larger ones — administer significant grant programs for local nonprofits, businesses, and community organizations.

**CDBG Subgrants**: Cities and counties that receive Community Development Block Grant (CDBG) entitlement funding often subgrant portions to local nonprofits through an annual competitive process. CDBG can fund a wide range of community development activities including housing rehabilitation, public services, economic development, and public facilities.

**City Arts and Culture Grants**: Many cities fund arts programs through dedicated arts commissions or cultural affairs offices. New York City, Los Angeles, Chicago, Seattle, Denver, and many other cities have robust local arts grant programs with their own budgets and priorities.

**Small Business and Economic Development Grants**: City economic development offices often administer small business grants, microenterprise programs, façade improvement grants, and Main Street revitalization funds. These are frequently underutilized because they're not well publicized outside local business communities.

**Housing and Homelessness**: City and county housing authorities administer HUD-funded programs for affordable housing, homeless services, and housing stability. Local Continuum of Care (CoC) grants flow through lead agencies to local service providers.

## Community Foundations: The Hidden Local Funder

Community foundations are one of the most powerful yet least known sources of local grant funding. Nearly every city, county, and region in the United States has a community foundation — an organization that pools charitable assets from local donors and families and re-grants them to organizations serving the community.

Community foundations typically have:

- Deep knowledge of and commitment to local needs and priorities
- Donor-advised funds that can be directed to local causes
- Competitive grant programs and scholarship programs
- Capacity-building grants for local nonprofits
- Special initiatives tied to local priorities like education, workforce, health equity, or environmental sustainability

The competition for community foundation grants is typically far lower than for national foundation funding, and relationships with program officers are more accessible. Local organizations with strong community roots and deep local impact are positioned to compete effectively for community foundation funding.

To find your local community foundation, search the Council on Foundations' community foundation locator or simply search "[your city or county] community foundation."

## Regional Foundations and Multi-State Funders

Between national foundations and purely local funders lies a middle tier of regional foundations that focus on specific geographic areas — the Southeast, the Mountain West, the Great Lakes region, or multi-state areas like Appalachia.

These regional foundations often fund issues with geographic specificity — environmental restoration in a particular watershed, economic development in a specific rural corridor, or healthcare in a defined multi-state region. Because their focus is geographic as well as topical, organizations operating in their region have a natural advantage.

Examples include the Mary Black Foundation (South Carolina), the Northwest Area Foundation (eight-state region), the Appalachian Regional Commission (federal/state partnership for Appalachia), and hundreds of others. Research foundations active in your specific region as part of a comprehensive local and regional funding strategy.

## Building a Location-Based Funding Map

A location-based funding map is a strategic tool that catalogs all the grant resources available to organizations in your specific geography. Building one involves:

**Tier 1 — Federal Geographic Programs**: Identify federal programs for which your location creates eligibility or priority (rural designations, Opportunity Zones, tribal status, etc.).

**Tier 2 — State Programs**: Research all relevant state agency grant programs in your state, organized by sector.

**Tier 3 — Regional Programs**: Identify regional foundations, multi-state initiatives, and federal regional programs (like EDA Economic Development Districts) covering your geography.

**Tier 4 — City and County Programs**: Research all municipal and county grant programs in your service area.

**Tier 5 — Community Foundations**: Identify all community foundations serving your area and research their current grant priorities.

**Tier 6 — Local Corporate Giving**: Research major employers in your area and their corporate giving programs, which are often locally focused.

This map becomes the foundation of your geographic funding strategy — a living document you update as programs change and new opportunities emerge.

## Using Geography as a Storytelling Tool

Beyond eligibility, geography is a storytelling asset in grant applications. Funders who prioritize your region want to know that you understand the specific context in which you work. Use local data. Reference community-specific challenges and assets. Name the neighborhoods, the counties, and the communities you serve.

Proposals that demonstrate deep geographic knowledge — that cite local census data, reference recent community needs assessments, and describe the specific landscape of services in a defined area — are more compelling and more credible than proposals that treat geography as an afterthought.

Where you are is part of who you are. Make it work for you.
